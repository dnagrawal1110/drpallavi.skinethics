THE BIGGER PICTURE

These are not skin diseases.

"Psoriasis and eczema patients come to me having tried 5 different creams and 3 different dermatologists. They've managed symptoms. But the flares keep coming back. The reason is that we've been treating the expression of a problem, not the problem itself. These are systemic conditions that happen to show on skin."

Psoriasis, eczema, and alopecia areata are classified by modern immunology as immune-mediated inflammatory disorders. Skin is where they show. But the immune dysregulation driving them originates elsewhere — specifically, in the gut-immune system that controls 70–80% of your body's entire inflammatory response.

This guide explains why your skin keeps flaring, what is actually happening in your gut and immune system, and the root-cause approach that addresses the origin — not just the surface expression.

THE GUT–SKIN AXIS — THREE PATHWAYS

The gut and skin are in constant conversation.

This is not metaphor — it is physiology. The gut-skin axis is a well-characterised bidirectional communication network with three distinct pathways. Disruption in any one of them triggers or amplifies inflammatory skin disease.

1. Immune Pathway

70–80% of all immune cells in the body reside in gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT). When the gut is dysbiotic or the gut barrier is compromised, GALT generates a pro-inflammatory immune signal that travels systemically — reaching skin and activating the keratinocyte inflammatory cascade that drives psoriasis plaques and eczema flares.

2. Metabolic Pathway

Gut bacteria metabolise dietary compounds to produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), lipopolysaccharides, and other metabolites that directly influence skin cell behaviour. A dysbiotic gut — high in pro-inflammatory bacteria, low in beneficial ones — produces an inflammatory metabolite profile that the skin responds to with increased cytokine production and barrier dysfunction.

3. Neuroendocrine Pathway (Gut-Brain-Skin Axis)

Stress signals from the brain alter gut permeability through cortisol and neuropeptide release. A stressed gut produces a leaky gut. A leaky gut produces a skin flare. This is the clinical reason why stress so reliably triggers psoriasis and eczema exacerbations — the pathway from emotional stress to skin inflammation is physiologically direct and well-documented.

HOW LEAKY GUT DRIVES SKIN CONDITIONS

This is the step-by-step mechanism from gut lining damage to skin flare. Understanding this pathway is what changes how patients approach their treatment — from symptom management to root-cause repair.

Gut lining is damaged

Trigger: infection, chronic stress, food sensitivities, repeated antibiotics, NSAIDs, or processed food-heavy diet.

Tight junctions between gut cells loosen

The intestinal epithelium normally functions as a selective barrier. Damaged tight junctions allow paracellular passage of molecules that should not enter circulation.

Food particles, toxins, and bacteria enter bloodstream

Bacterial lipopolysaccharides, undigested food antigens, and endotoxins cross the compromised barrier — triggering "leaky gut" or intestinal hyperpermeability.

Immune system enters panic mode

GALT and systemic immune cells identify these foreign molecules and mount an inflammatory response — including autoimmune reactivity when the antigens resemble the body's own tissue.

Pro-inflammatory cytokines rise

IL-17, TNF-α, IL-23, and IL-22 — the cytokines driving psoriasis plaques and eczema — are produced in elevated quantities. Biologic medications target exactly these cytokines at the symptom end; root cause work addresses why they are elevated in the first place.

Skin shows the result

Psoriasis plaques (accelerated keratinocyte turnover), eczema flares (barrier breakdown + inflammatory infiltration), alopecia patches (follicular immune attack). The skin is not the disease — it is the display board.

Despite different immune mechanisms, both conditions share the same root cause architecture: a gut that is generating a disproportionate and misdirected inflammatory signal. The difference is in which downstream immune pathway becomes dominant — but the upstream source is the same furnace.

WHAT INFLAMES THE GUT — COMMON TRIGGERS
⚠ Refined sugar and processed foods — feed pro-inflammatory bacterial species (Proteobacteria) while starving beneficial ones (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium), shifting the microbiome balance toward a dysbiotic, inflammatory profile.
⚠ Dairy sensitivity — casein protein can trigger immune reactivity in predisposed individuals, generating the same antigen-antibody cycle that ultimately amplifies skin inflammation.
⚠ Gluten sensitivity (non-coeliac) — gliadin protein triggers zonulin release, which directly loosens intestinal tight junctions. Psoriasis has documented correlation with gluten sensitivity in peer-reviewed research.
⚠ Chronic stress — cortisol directly damages gut tight junctions, independently of diet. This is why mental health management is a clinical tool in inflammatory skin disease, not a lifestyle recommendation.
⚠ NSAIDs and antibiotics — both disrupt gut microbiome composition. NSAIDs also directly damage gut epithelium. Repeated courses correlate with worsening skin inflammation over time in susceptible individuals.
⚠ Poor and disrupted sleep — the gut microbiome has its own circadian rhythm. Consistently disrupted sleep patterns change microbiome composition toward a pro-inflammatory profile within 2–3 days of disruption.
THE ROOT CAUSE APPROACH

Root cause work and skin symptom management are not mutually exclusive — they happen simultaneously. Dr. Pallavi manages the skin while you work on the upstream causes. The goal is to progressively reduce the inflammatory signal from the gut so that skin maintenance becomes easier and eventual remission becomes possible.

1

Elimination trial — dairy and gluten

Remove dairy and gluten for 4–6 weeks. If there is significant improvement in skin condition, a sensitivity is confirmed. This is the lowest-cost, highest-information diagnostic step available and requires no blood test.

2

Gut lining repair

L-glutamine (5g/day) provides the amino acid most used by intestinal epithelial cells for repair. Zinc carnosine supports tight junction integrity. Bone broth provides collagen precursors and glycine. These are evidence-supported, not experimental.

3

Probiotics — strain-specific

Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG and L. acidophilus have the strongest evidence base for reducing gut dysbiosis in inflammatory skin disease. Fermented foods (curd, idli, dosa, kanji) provide natural probiotic support alongside supplementation.

4

Anti-inflammatory dietary pattern

Increase omega-3 rich foods (fish, flaxseed, walnuts). Reduce glycaemic load. Increase fibre from diverse plant sources — this feeds the beneficial microbiome. The Mediterranean dietary pattern has the strongest evidence base for reducing inflammatory skin disease activity.

5

Stress management — clinical priority

Not optional, not a lifestyle choice. Cortisol management is a therapeutic intervention. Journaling, breathwork, progressive muscle relaxation, moderate exercise — any consistent practice that measurably reduces cortisol. The gut-skin axis runs directly through stress physiology.

6

Concurrent skin management under Dr. Pallavi

Root cause work takes 6–12 weeks to show measurable gut and skin improvement. During this period, Dr. Pallavi manages skin symptoms to prevent progressive damage, reduce infection risk, and maintain quality of life while the upstream work proceeds.

WHY STEROIDS ALONE FAIL — THE HONEST ANSWER

Steroids manage. They don't heal.

Topical and oral steroids are powerful anti-inflammatory agents. They suppress the immune response at the skin — quickly and effectively. A psoriasis plaque can clear in 2 weeks on potent topical steroids. An eczema flare can resolve in days.

But steroids do not address gut permeability. They do not restore microbiome balance. They do not change the cytokine signal being generated upstream. When steroids are reduced or stopped, the inflammatory signal from the gut resumes and the skin flares again — often rebounding harder than before long-term topical steroid use.

This is why so many patients are on steroids indefinitely. Not because steroids are the right long-term answer — but because no one has addressed the reason the inflammation exists in the first place. The conversation about gut health, diet, stress, and microbiome is not an alternative to dermatological care. It is the missing component that makes dermatological care finally work long-term.
