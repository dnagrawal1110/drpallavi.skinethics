THE PHONE BATTERY ANALOGY

Your Body Goes Into Power Saver Mode

When your phone reaches 1% battery, it activates Power Saver Mode — it dims the screen, stops background apps, reduces processor speed, and redirects all remaining energy to only the most critical functions: phone calls, alarms, the things that matter for survival.

Your body does exactly this when it experiences a sudden drop in calories or nutrition. The critical functions that continue at full power: heart function, brain activity, immune response, temperature regulation. The background apps that get switched off: hair follicle activity. Hair is biologically classified as non-essential. During a nutritional emergency, the body stops routing energy and nutrients to follicles because surviving is more important than looking good.

This response is not a malfunction — it's the body working exactly as it should. The problem is that it happens silently, the effect is delayed by 2–6 months (causing maximum confusion), and most people never connect their hair fall to the diet change that triggered it months earlier.

THE TIMELINE — WHY YOU'RE BLAMING THE WRONG CAUSE

The 3-Month Delay That Confuses Everyone

Here is the typical story: someone goes on a strict diet in January. They lose 8–10 kg by March. They feel great and consider the diet complete. Then in May or June — hair fall begins. Massive shedding. They come to the clinic in July, several months after the diet ended, convinced the hair fall is unrelated to the diet. "I stopped dieting months ago."

This is the exact trajectory of Telogen Effluvium. The hair follicle doesn't immediately shut down when you start a crash diet — it takes 2–3 months for the follicle to move from the active growth phase (anagen) into the resting/shedding phase (telogen). Then, another 2–3 months later, those resting hairs all shed simultaneously. The delay is physiological, predictable, and almost universally misattributed.

The confusion is compounded by the fact that you feel perfectly fine — you've already resumed normal eating. Your body looks recovered. But the follicles set into telogen phase months ago are now completing their cycle and shedding on schedule, completely indifferent to what you ate last week.

The delay also works in reverse — recovery takes 3–6 months after the correct nutritional support is in place, because follicles need time to move back into the anagen (growth) phase.
"Telogen Effluvium is not a disease. It's your body doing exactly what it should — protecting vital organs over cosmetic functions. The question is: how do you signal to it that the emergency is over?"

The signal is specific, measurable nutrients — not general "healthy eating." Blood tests identify exactly what is depleted, and targeted supplementation gives the body what it needs to move follicles back from rest into active growth.

HIGH RISK TRIGGERS FOR TELOGEN EFFLUVIUM

Crash diets under 1,000 cal/day

Severe caloric restriction is the most common trigger. The body interprets this as a famine and immediately begins resource-rationing to vital organs.

Protein-restricted diets

Hair is made of keratin — pure protein. Any diet that eliminates or severely restricts protein (certain vegan approaches without careful planning, meal replacement diets) directly removes the building material for hair.

Post-bariatric surgery

Rapid body composition change combined with altered nutrient absorption is a very high-risk combination. Hair fall 3–6 months post-bariatric surgery is extremely common and well-documented.

Extended keto or intermittent fasting without nutritional support

Both can work safely with proper nutrient monitoring, but done aggressively without tracking iron, zinc, and protein — they are frequent TE triggers.

Post-illness recovery

Any significant illness — including prolonged COVID-19 — can trigger TE through a combination of the physiological stress response and nutritional depletion during illness.

Post-pregnancy

The combination of hormonal drop (estrogen falls rapidly after delivery) and nutritional depletion from pregnancy/lactation makes post-partum TE extremely common, typically peaking at 3–4 months postpartum.

THE RESCUE PROTOCOL — STEP BY STEP
1

Serum Ferritin Test — Most Important First Step

Target >50 ng/mL. Serum ferritin is the most common deficiency in post-diet hair fall — and is frequently normal on standard CBC while ferritin is depleted. Ask specifically for ferritin, not just haemoglobin.

2

Iron Supplementation if Ferritin is Low

Always take iron with Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) for absorption. Take on empty stomach for maximum absorption, or with food if it causes gastric upset. Avoid with tea/coffee within 1 hour (tannins block absorption).

3

Zinc Supplementation

Zinc is depleted rapidly during caloric restriction and is essential for follicle function and protein synthesis. Serum zinc can appear normal while intracellular zinc is insufficient — supplementing 15–30 mg/day is often warranted.

4

Protein Reintroduction — 1.2–1.5g per kg Body Weight

Hair is keratin — it is made of protein. Meeting minimum protein requirements is non-negotiable for recovery. Dal, paneer, eggs, curd, legumes, and nuts are all valid sources for Indian diets.

5

Biotin — Only If Deficiency is Confirmed

Biotin is rarely the actual problem in TE. Most Indians eating a varied diet (dal, eggs, nuts, green vegetables) are not biotin-deficient. Adding biotin supplements without checking other levels treats the wrong thing. See the Biotin guide (comment VITAMIN) for the full picture.

6

Patience — The Recovery Takes 3–6 Months

Once the correct nutritional support is in place, follicles need time to exit telogen and re-enter the anagen growth phase. Visible improvement in hair fall reduction typically starts at 6–8 weeks, with density improvement at 3–6 months. This timeline is biological — it cannot be rushed significantly.

WHAT NOT TO DO — COMMON MISTAKES
✕

Taking random hair supplements without blood tests

You cannot guess which nutrient is depleted. Taking biotin when your ferritin is the problem wastes months. Test first, then supplement specifically.

✕

Applying more hair oil to compensate

External oil application addresses neither the nutritional deficit nor the follicle shutdown. The root cause is internal — scalp massage and oiling are not substitutes for nutrition.

✕

Resuming the crash diet

The most counterproductive response to TE hair fall is restricting calories further. The body will intensify the power-saver response, and the hair fall will worsen or become chronic.

✕

Ignoring it and hoping it resolves

Untreated TE can persist or progress, especially if there is an underlying hormonal issue co-existing. Beyond 6 months, it warrants investigation. Earlier intervention means earlier resolution.

When to Seek a Consultation

If hair fall continues beyond 6 months after resuming normal nutrition with adequate protein and supplementation for confirmed deficiencies — this warrants a full hormonal and thyroid panel. A significant number of cases have an underlying hormonal trigger (thyroid dysfunction, PCOS, androgen excess) that is co-existing with and amplifying the nutritional Telogen Effluvium.

Similarly: if hair fall is accompanied by fatigue, cold intolerance, weight changes, irregular periods, or skin changes — a thyroid panel should be run simultaneously with nutritional tests. These symptoms point toward a systemic cause beyond diet alone.
